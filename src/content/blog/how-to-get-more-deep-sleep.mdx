Deep sleep is when your body does its most critical repair work. It is the phase of sleep where tissues regenerate, the immune system strengthens, and growth hormone floods the bloodstream. Without enough of it, you wake up feeling groggy no matter how many hours you spent in bed, your workouts stop producing results, and your ability to fight off illness drops sharply.

The problem is that deep sleep is also the most fragile stage of sleep. Alcohol, stress, an overly warm bedroom, or even a slightly irregular schedule can cut into it without you ever realizing what happened. Many people who sleep seven or eight hours a night are still chronically short on deep sleep.

If you have been wondering how to get more deep sleep, you are not alone. It is one of the most common sleep quality concerns, and fortunately, the science points to several concrete strategies that work. This guide covers what deep sleep actually is, how much you need, why it tends to decline over time, and ten evidence-based ways to increase it starting tonight.

## What Is Deep Sleep?

Deep sleep, also called slow-wave sleep or stage 3 NREM sleep, is the third stage of non-rapid eye movement sleep in the modern sleep staging model used by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. During this stage, your brain produces high-amplitude, low-frequency electrical waves called delta waves, oscillating at roughly 0.5 to 4 Hz.

Several things distinguish deep sleep from lighter stages:

- **Heart rate and breathing slow significantly.** Both reach their lowest sustained levels of the night during deep sleep.
- **Blood pressure drops.** This nightly dip is important for cardiovascular health, and insufficient deep sleep has been linked to sustained high blood pressure.
- **Growth hormone is released.** The pituitary gland releases the majority of its daily growth hormone output during deep sleep, which is essential for muscle repair, bone density maintenance, and cellular regeneration.
- **The brain clears metabolic waste.** The glymphatic system, which removes toxic byproducts like beta-amyloid from the brain, is most active during slow-wave sleep. Research published in *Science* has linked impaired glymphatic clearance to increased Alzheimer's disease risk.
- **It is very difficult to wake someone up.** If you are roused during deep sleep, you will likely feel disoriented and groggy for several minutes, a phenomenon called sleep inertia.

Most deep sleep occurs during the first half of the night, concentrated in the first two or three sleep cycles. This is why going to bed on time matters more for deep sleep than sleeping in does.

## How Much Deep Sleep Do You Need?

For healthy adults, deep sleep typically accounts for about 13 to 23 percent of total sleep time. In practical terms, that translates to roughly 1 to 2 hours per night if you are getting the recommended 7 to 9 hours of total sleep.

The National Sleep Foundation does not set a strict minimum for deep sleep the way it does for total sleep duration, partly because deep sleep is difficult to measure without polysomnography (a clinical sleep study). However, consistently falling below one hour of deep sleep per night is associated with daytime fatigue, impaired memory consolidation, and weakened immune function.

## Why Deep Sleep Declines With Age

One of the most well-documented findings in sleep research is that deep sleep decreases naturally as you age. A landmark study published in *Neuron* found that slow-wave activity begins declining in the late twenties and drops steadily from there. By age 60, many people get 60 to 70 percent less deep sleep than they did at age 25.

Several factors drive this decline:

- **Reduced neuronal synchrony.** The large, coordinated delta waves that define deep sleep require tight synchronization among cortical neurons. Aging reduces this synchronization.
- **Changes in sleep architecture.** Older adults tend to have more fragmented sleep with more frequent awakenings, which disrupts the consolidation of deep sleep episodes.
- **Lower adenosine sensitivity.** Adenosine is a key driver of sleep pressure, and the brain's sensitivity to it appears to diminish with age.

This decline is not entirely preventable, but it can be slowed and partially offset through the strategies outlined below. The fact that deep sleep decreases naturally makes it even more important to avoid the behaviors that suppress it further.

## 10 Ways to Get More Deep Sleep

### 1. Exercise Earlier in the Day

Exercise is one of the most consistently supported strategies for increasing deep sleep. A meta-analysis published in *Sleep Medicine Reviews* found that regular moderate-to-vigorous exercise increased slow-wave sleep by a meaningful margin across multiple studies.

The timing matters. Vigorous exercise raises core body temperature, increases cortisol, and stimulates the sympathetic nervous system, all of which are counterproductive to falling into deep sleep. Aim to finish intense workouts at least 4 to 6 hours before bedtime. Morning or early afternoon exercise tends to produce the best deep sleep outcomes because it gives your body enough time to cool down and shift into parasympathetic mode before bed.

Even 30 minutes of brisk walking or cycling can make a measurable difference if done consistently. The key is regularity. A single workout may not dramatically change your deep sleep that night, but a consistent exercise habit over several weeks reliably does.

### 2. Keep Your Bedroom Cool

Your body needs to drop its core temperature by about 1 to 1.5 degrees Celsius to initiate and maintain deep sleep. A warm bedroom works directly against this process.

Research from the National Institutes of Health and multiple sleep labs has identified 60 to 67 degrees Fahrenheit (15.5 to 19.4 degrees Celsius) as the optimal bedroom temperature range for most adults. Within that range, 65 to 68 degrees Fahrenheit tends to be the sweet spot for maximizing slow-wave sleep.

Practical ways to cool your sleep environment include using breathable bedding materials like cotton or linen, keeping a fan running for air circulation, and taking a warm shower 60 to 90 minutes before bed. The warm shower might sound counterintuitive, but it triggers a thermoregulatory response: your body rapidly cools itself afterward, accelerating the core temperature drop that promotes deep sleep.

### 3. Avoid Alcohol Before Bed

Alcohol is one of the most misunderstood sleep disruptors. It is a sedative, so it does help you fall asleep faster. But sedation is not the same as sleep, and the trade-off is severe: alcohol fragments the second half of the night and dramatically reduces deep sleep.

A 2018 study published in *JMIR Mental Health* involving over 4,000 participants found that even moderate alcohol consumption (two drinks for men, one for women) reduced sleep quality by 24 percent. High alcohol consumption reduced it by nearly 40 percent. Deep sleep and REM sleep were the stages most affected.

The mechanism is straightforward. As your liver metabolizes alcohol during the night, it produces aldehydes and triggers sympathetic nervous system activation. This pulls you out of deep sleep and into lighter stages. If you are serious about how to increase deep sleep, limiting alcohol to earlier in the evening or eliminating it on most nights is one of the highest-impact changes you can make.

### 4. Maintain a Consistent Sleep Schedule

Your circadian rhythm governs the timing of sleep stages. Deep sleep is concentrated in the first few hours after sleep onset, and the timing of that window is calibrated to your habitual bedtime. When you shift your bedtime significantly from night to night, your circadian system cannot optimize the placement of deep sleep within your cycles.

Research from Harvard Medical School has shown that irregular sleep schedules are associated with reduced slow-wave sleep, even when total sleep duration remains adequate. The participants who kept the most consistent schedules had the highest proportion of deep sleep.

Going to bed and waking up within a 30-minute window every day, including weekends, is one of the simplest and most effective strategies for protecting deep sleep. ### 5. Get Enough Total Sleep

This one seems obvious, but it is worth stating explicitly: you cannot get enough deep sleep if you are not sleeping long enough overall. Deep sleep typically occurs in the first two to three sleep cycles, but cutting total sleep short reduces the overall opportunity for your brain to complete and consolidate those cycles.

Someone sleeping only five or six hours a night may still get some deep sleep in their early cycles, but the total amount is almost always lower than someone sleeping seven to eight hours. The relationship is not perfectly linear, but the data is clear that short sleepers consistently report less deep sleep on wearable trackers and in sleep lab studies.

Use our [sleep calculator](/) to find the right bedtime based on when you need to wake up. Aligning your schedule with full 90-minute sleep cycles gives your body the best chance of completing its deep sleep phases without interruption.

### 6. Reduce Evening Screen Time

The blue light emitted by phones, tablets, and computer screens suppresses melatonin production. Melatonin does not directly cause deep sleep, but it plays a role in the cascade of neurochemical events that initiate sleep onset and set up the conditions for slow-wave sleep in the first cycles.

A study conducted at Brigham and Women's Hospital found that participants who used light-emitting devices before bed had suppressed melatonin levels, delayed sleep onset, reduced REM sleep, and reported feeling less alert the next morning. While the study focused on REM, the delayed sleep onset it caused also compressed the early-night deep sleep window.

The practical recommendation is to stop using screens at least 30 to 60 minutes before bed. If that is not feasible, use night mode settings that shift the display toward warmer tones, or wear blue-light-blocking glasses. However, simply putting the phone down remains the most effective option.

### 7. Try Pink Noise or White Noise

One of the more intriguing areas of deep sleep research involves acoustic stimulation. A study published in *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* found that playing gentle pink noise pulses timed to the brain's slow-wave oscillations increased the power and duration of deep sleep in participants.

Pink noise differs from white noise in that it emphasizes lower frequencies, creating a deeper, more even sound similar to steady rain or a distant waterfall. The theory is that these low-frequency sounds resonate with the delta waves produced during deep sleep, reinforcing and prolonging them.

You do not need specialized equipment to try this. A simple pink noise track played at low volume through a bedside speaker can be enough. Several studies have replicated the effect, though the magnitude varies. At a minimum, consistent background noise masks environmental disruptions like traffic or a partner's movements that might fragment your deep sleep.

### 8. Manage Stress and Anxiety

Cortisol, the primary stress hormone, directly antagonizes deep sleep. When cortisol levels remain elevated in the evening, the brain has difficulty transitioning from lighter NREM stages into slow-wave sleep. Chronic stress keeps the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis activated, creating a state of physiological arousal that is incompatible with the deep relaxation deep sleep requires.

Effective stress management techniques that have been studied in the context of sleep include:

- **Progressive muscle relaxation.** Systematically tensing and releasing muscle groups reduces physical tension and cortisol levels before bed.
- **Meditation and mindfulness.** A 2015 study in *JAMA Internal Medicine* found that mindfulness meditation improved sleep quality in older adults, with participants showing improvements in sleep depth.
- **Journaling.** Writing down worries or a to-do list for the next day has been shown to reduce pre-sleep cognitive arousal.
- **Deep breathing exercises.** Slow diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, directly countering the cortisol-driven arousal that blocks deep sleep.

The key is consistency. A five-minute breathing exercise done every night before bed will do more for your deep sleep over time than an occasional hour-long meditation session.

### 9. Watch Your Caffeine Cutoff

Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, and adenosine is one of the primary neurochemical drivers of sleep pressure, which is what pushes your brain into deep sleep. Even if caffeine does not prevent you from falling asleep, it can significantly reduce the amount of slow-wave sleep you achieve.

A study published in the *Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine* found that caffeine consumed six hours before bedtime still reduced total sleep time by more than one hour and significantly impacted sleep depth. Caffeine's half-life in most adults is 5 to 7 hours, but this varies based on genetics, liver enzyme activity, and other factors.

Our [caffeine cutoff calculator](/calculators/caffeine-cutoff) can help you find the right time to stop drinking coffee or tea based on your specific bedtime. For most people, a cutoff of early to mid-afternoon is sufficient, but if you are particularly sensitive to caffeine, you may need to stop even earlier.

### 10. Consider Magnesium and Glycine

Two supplements have accumulated meaningful evidence for supporting deep sleep, though neither should be considered a replacement for the behavioral strategies above.

**Magnesium** plays a role in regulating GABA, the neurotransmitter that promotes relaxation and sleep. A randomized controlled trial published in the *Journal of Research in Medical Sciences* found that magnesium supplementation (500 mg daily) improved subjective sleep quality and increased sleep time in elderly participants. Magnesium glycinate and magnesium threonate are the forms most commonly recommended for sleep, as they cross the blood-brain barrier more effectively than magnesium oxide.

**Glycine**, an amino acid, has been shown in several Japanese studies to improve subjective sleep quality and reduce daytime fatigue. A study in *Sleep and Biological Rhythms* found that 3 grams of glycine taken before bed lowered core body temperature and promoted faster entry into deep sleep.

Important caveats: supplement quality varies widely, these studies are generally small, and individual responses differ. Consult a healthcare provider before starting any supplement, especially if you take medications or have existing health conditions. This is informational only and is not medical advice.

## How to Track Your Deep Sleep

Consumer wearable devices like Oura Ring, Fitbit, WHOOP, and Apple Watch all estimate sleep stages, including deep sleep. They use a combination of heart rate variability, movement data, and in some cases skin temperature to classify your time in each stage.

These estimates are not as accurate as clinical polysomnography, which uses EEG electrodes to measure brain waves directly. However, they are useful for tracking trends over time. If your wearable consistently shows your deep sleep increasing after you start exercising regularly or lower your bedroom temperature, that trend data is meaningful even if the absolute minute counts are approximate.

The most actionable approach is to track deep sleep as a percentage of total sleep time rather than focusing on exact minutes. If you are consistently in the 13 to 23 percent range and your number is trending upward as you implement changes, you are moving in the right direction.

If you are building a [sleep debt](/calculators/sleep-debt) from consistently short nights, addressing total sleep duration first will often improve your deep sleep numbers as a downstream effect.

## When to See a Doctor

If you have implemented the strategies above consistently for several weeks and are still experiencing symptoms of poor deep sleep, such as chronic fatigue despite adequate sleep duration, difficulty concentrating, frequent illness, or very low deep sleep readings on a reliable wearable, it is worth consulting a sleep specialist.

Certain medical conditions can specifically impair deep sleep:

- **Sleep apnea** causes repeated micro-awakenings that fragment deep sleep, often without the sleeper being aware.
- **Restless leg syndrome** creates arousal events that prevent the brain from settling into slow-wave stages.
- **Chronic pain conditions** activate the sympathetic nervous system and elevate cortisol, both of which suppress deep sleep.
- **Certain medications**, including some antidepressants, beta-blockers, and corticosteroids, can reduce slow-wave sleep as a side effect.

A sleep specialist can order a polysomnography study that provides definitive data on your sleep architecture, including exact deep sleep duration and any disruptions.
